The Initiative

Tackling a behavioral health epidemic — systemically

Behavioral health conditions affect tens of millions of Americans, yet the systems designed to help them — clinical, financial, and organizational — are fundamentally misaligned. At Highmark Health, I led the design strategy phase of a cross-enterprise innovation initiative focused on defining the problems to solve, building a strategic framework to align all Behavioral Health efforts, and recommending opportunities for innovation.

The work involved extensive research with internal subject matter experts, competitive analysis, and synthesis of secondary data across six interconnected problem areas. The output was a strategic framework and a prioritized set of innovation opportunities to guide Highmark's long-term behavioral health investments.

Strategic Framework

A vision to be the industry catalyst for behavioral health

The strategic framework organized Highmark's Behavioral Health ambitions across four pillars: changing the dialog around mental health to reduce stigma, innovating the value chain from prevention through treatment, optimizing clinical and community capacity, and fixing the underlying business model to enable long-term investment in care.

Near-term priorities focused on the highest-cost, most acute needs — where the path to impact was clearest. Longer-term, the vision was to convene the academic, clinical, and innovation community around more systemic, cost-effective solutions for sustaining and restoring mental health at scale.

Research Synthesis

Six Interconnected Problem Areas

Through extensive interviews with internal SMEs and secondary research, we identified six problem areas in behavioral health that are complex and deeply interconnected.

1
Remove Barriers to Value Realization

Low reimbursement, fragmented data, and systemic payer barriers suppress care — despite the 2008 Mental Health Parity Act.

2
Address Clinician Challenges

High caseloads, low reimbursement, evolving best practices, and systemic bias prevent clinicians from delivering their best care.

3
Match Individual Need with the Right Care

8–10 years average delay from symptom onset to first treatment. Co-morbidity, misdiagnosis, and access gaps compound the problem.

4
Shift from Reactive to Proactive

56% of adults with mental illness receive no treatment. Predictive tools and proactive outreach are nearly nonexistent.

Stigma is a barrier to seeking care, a barrier within clinical environments, and compounded by social determinants of health — including housing, food insecurity, and economic instability.

Innovation Opportunities

Prioritizing for impact and feasibility

After synthesizing research across problem areas, the team identified and prioritized a set of innovation opportunities mapped across impact and effort. Priority opportunities were developed with full summaries, competitive landscape analysis, and recommended next steps for scoping and implementation.

Clinicians as Whole-Health Practitioners

Expand the role of non-Behavioral Health specialists to "whole-health practitioners" — enabling all clinicians to confidently engage patients about behavioral and physical health wellness without adding administrative burden.

Communities as Caregivers

Leverage community momentum — schools, employers, families, nonprofits — as a first line of defense for early identification, support, and stigma reduction at scale.

Flipping the Risk Problem

Reframe the investment risk of Behavioral Health care by exploring new payer/provider business models that allow realization of the longer-term value of Behavioral Health outcomes.

Use Data for Insights & Action

Intentionally design a data collection and analytics program to identify at-risk individuals, measure Behavioral Health intervention outcomes, and drive proactive care.

Open Behavioral Health Resources for All

Accelerate virtual, peer-to-peer, and community access models — reaching the 60%+ of counties with no psychiatrist and the 94M Americans waiting more than a week for mental health services.

Respond to COVID-19 Behavioral Health Needs

Address the wave of emerging and exacerbated Behavioral Health conditions from the pandemic — isolation, grief, trauma, and fear — before they become high-cost, chronic conditions.

Transformational Innovation

The Behavioral Health Care Journey We're Designing For

A transformational approach means shifting from reactive, crisis-driven care to a full-spectrum journey — from staying well through sustained recovery.

1
Prevention
Living with Intent to Stay Well

Building habits, community, and conditions that support mental wellness before a crisis occurs.

2
Recognition
Becoming Aware of the Need for Care

Identifying when something is wrong — through self-awareness, clinical screening, or community support.

3
Activation
Seeking & Pursuing Care

Navigating access — overcoming stigma, cost, and availability barriers to get matched with the right care.

4
Treatment
Participating in Care

Receiving evidence-based interventions, therapy, or medication — with continuity and appropriate clinical support.

5
Management
Managing Ongoing Wellness & Recovery

Sustaining recovery through ongoing check-ins, relapse prevention, and integrated whole-health support.

Current gap

Today, the system is concentrated in stages 4–5 (treatment and management). The innovation opportunity is in stages 1–3 — prevention, early recognition, and seamless activation — where intervention can prevent escalation to crisis care.

Phase 2

From problem space to solution concepts

With the strategic framework established and two innovation pillars selected by senior leadership — Maintain Behavioral Health and Optimize Capacity — the work moved into Phase 2: scoping the best opportunities Highmark could pursue and designing concrete, scored solution concepts for leadership alignment and funding approval.

Phase 2 Research

30+ interviews, 6 expert conversations, and 80+ ideation participants

Phase 2 engaged in deep research — 30+ clinician interviews across AHN and partner organizations, 30+ customer interviews and concept tests, 6 conversations with external experts including Forrester analysts, startups, and national advocacy groups, and 6 collaborative ideation sessions with 80+ cross-enterprise and external participants.

Starting from six prioritized opportunity areas, the team generated 12 solution concepts, scored each against impact, difficulty, and scalability, and narrowed to the five highest-potential concepts to present for leadership alignment and funding approval.

Foundational Concept

The Behavioral Health Care Hub as a platform, not a product

The cornerstone of the solution concepts is the Behavioral Health Hub — a consolidation and curation point for behavioral health solutions, built on the Living Health Platform and integrated seamlessly into the broader customer and clinician experience. The design intent was explicit: this is not a standalone app.

The Behavioral Health Hub isn't the Highmark app. It isn't Highmark.com or MyStrength or the EMR or Virtual Care. It connects and integrates with all of these to create a single, simple access point for people seeking Behavioral Health care and resources — and a launching point for the four other concepts to plug into.

Concept Deep-Dives

What each concept addresses and how it creates value

Behavioral Health Care Hub

Score 8.73

A digital resource hub making Behavioral Health care access simple and non-intimidating — consolidating provider scheduling, wellness tools, peer groups, and real-time telehealth in one experience. Nationally scalable with a first-mover commercialization opportunity for provider partners and other Blues plans. Investment: $$.

Perpetual Screening

Score 9.09

Simplified, staged screening tools deployed across the entire clinical footprint — enabling non-Behavioral Health clinicians to recognize onset, and patients to self-screen privately at home via SMS or the Behavioral Health Hub. Becomes the scalable structure for early intervention. Investment: $$.

Peer Support — Anywhere

Score 9.49

The highest-scoring concept. An offline/online platform connecting individuals with peers who share similar experiences — with peer counselors as champions who bridge support and clinical care. A low-cost, highly effective mode of care with national scaling potential via partners. Investment: $$$.

Navigational Pathways

Score 8.89

A cross-system digital "Sherpa" educating patients, caregivers, and providers along the care continuum — with Behavioral Health-specific discharge plans, care pathway management, and condition management tools. Empowers non-Behavioral Health clinicians to propose care options confidently. Investment: $$$.

Culturally Competent Care

Score 8.73

Addressing the low efficacy of Behavioral Health care for African-American and other underserved populations through educational content, clinician certifications in race-based therapeutic approaches, and a longitudinal AHN Behavioral Health workforce diversification plan. The lowest-cost concept ($) with potential to improve care equity across the entire network.
