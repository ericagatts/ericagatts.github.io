The Challenge

Heart failure is the #1 cause of hospitalization in Medicare — and most cases go unmanaged.

Despite being one of the most costly and prevalent conditions in the United States, heart failure is systematically underidentified and undertreated. The vast majority of interventions happen reactively — after hospitalization — rather than proactively. This project set out to understand why, and where design could help.

Within Highmark's own member population, 27,165 distinct members had CHF inpatient stays in a single year. Of those, 76% did not have a primary CHF diagnosis — meaning the condition was frequently missed or secondary to other acute events. This underdiagnosis creates a critical window for proactive design intervention.

Ecosystem Assessment

Most existing solutions are reactive — intervening after hospitalization rather than before.

Patient Research

Insights from patient research

Primary research with CHF patients revealed a condition that touches every dimension of daily life — and demands a fundamentally different kind of care model than most chronic conditions.

CHF is a lifelong balancing act

Living with CHF is an ongoing journey requiring constant adjustment of diet, medication, activity, and emotional resilience — with little systemic support for long-term self-management.

Patients are motivated by normalcy

CHF patients are driven by the desire to live longer and maintain a "normal life" — to do what they want and need to do. Constraints on activity, diet, and lifestyle are both pain points and motivators.

Staying motivated is the hardest part

Maintaining consistent diet and exercise regimens is the single biggest non-emotional challenge of managing CHF — particularly over the long term without behavioral support systems.

CHF carries a uniquely heavy emotional burden

Compared to other chronic conditions, CHF places a much heavier emotional toll on patients — often requiring mental health support that the current care system rarely provides proactively.

Physical constraints reshape identity

Limitations on work, activity, and lifestyle aren't just inconveniences — they redefine who patients feel they are. Design solutions must account for the identity dimensions of the condition.

Clinician Research

Insights from clinician research

Seventeen clinician interviews — spanning case managers, primary care physicians, cardiologists, and hospitalists — revealed systemic gaps in how the care system is structured to support CHF patients.

Social determinants create significant access barriers

Social determinants of health — transportation, food security, housing — can significantly impact whether CHF patients get access to the care they need, often outside the clinical team's visibility.

Post-discharge experience is the key readmission lever

A successful transition home is directly tied to reducing the risk of readmission. Clinicians cite the first 30 days post-discharge as the highest-risk window — and the hardest to support remotely.

Remote monitoring is valuable but difficult to manage

Both providers and patients face real challenges with remote monitoring and CHF home management — from technology activation to alert fatigue and unclear escalation pathways.

Problems to Solve

How Might We…

Research synthesis surfaced critical design challenges across both the patient and clinician experience — translated into "How Might We" questions to guide concept development.

Patient Experience
Lifestyle & Motivation
…support CHF patients in staying motivated to make and sustain positive lifestyle changes — diet, exercise, and daily habits?
Affordability
…ensure that necessary medications and treatments are affordable and accessible for patients across socioeconomic backgrounds?
Data Sharing
…make it easy and valuable for patients to share their biometric data with providers in a way that doesn't feel burdensome?
Clinician Experience
Equity in Care
…enable clinicians to deliver the best possible care for the patient irrespective of their socio-economic condition or geography?
Early Identification
…make it easier for clinicians to identify potential issues or exacerbations in a virtual care setup and minimize the chance of readmission?
Continuum of Care
…give clinicians the confidence that their patient is staying connected to the care and support they need along the entire continuum?

Strategic Opportunity

Tiers of remote patient monitoring for CHF

Remote patient monitoring is critical to a CHF solutions strategy. Proactive outreach before exacerbations accelerate can meaningfully reduce hospitalizations, ED visits, and observation stays — but deployment complexity varies widely.

General PopulationSpecific Population
Easy to deployDifficult to deploy

Tier 1 · Broad Population

Passive and device-agnostic monitoring suitable for general CHF populations. Easy to activate; lower clinical specificity. Scale is the advantage.

Tier 2 · Targeted Monitoring

Condition-specific biometric tracking (weight, fluid status, activity). Requires patient activation and care team workflows to act on alerts.

Tier 3 · Implantable / Advanced

High-fidelity hemodynamic monitoring (e.g. CardioMEMS). Most clinically precise but limited to specific high-risk populations and complex to deploy.

"How might we manage all this data?" — The most critical design challenge in remote monitoring is not the sensors, but the data infrastructure, alert logic, and care team workflows that make the data actionable.
